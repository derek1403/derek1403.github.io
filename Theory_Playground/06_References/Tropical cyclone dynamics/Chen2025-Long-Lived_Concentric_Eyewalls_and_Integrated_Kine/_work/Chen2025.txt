Long‐Lived Concentric Eyewalls and Integrated Kinetic
Energy in Tropical Cyclones
Buo‐Fu Chen1
, Chia‐Le Chen2, and Hung‐Chi Kuo2
1Center for Weather and Climate Disaster Research, National Taiwan University, Taipei, Taiwan, 2Department of
Atmospheric Sciences, National Taiwan University, Taipei, Taiwan
Abstract Long‐lived concentric eyewalls (CEs) and short‐lived CEs are accompanied by diverse structural
parameters such as intensity (Vmax) and integrated kinetic energy (IKE). This study used a CE data set and an AI‐
based tropical cyclone (TC) wind profile reanalysis to examine the impact of CEs on TC evolution revealed by
IKE‐Vmax diagrams. AI‐based wind profiles are a fusion of multichannel satellite imagery and ERA5 data,
representing 0–750 km profiles of axisymmetric surface winds. The AI‐derived IKE has an R = 0.996 against
aircraft observations. Composite results show that short‐lived CEs (duration <20 h) tend to maintain IKE, while
long‐lived CEs (25% of all CEs) favor further IKE growth, contributing to TCs with an enormous circulation. It
is suggested that CEs may modulate the TC life cycle, evolving from small, intense storms to large, mature
storms to large, weak‐intensity storms before their ultimate demise.
Plain Language Summary Concentric eyewalls (CEs) occur in TCs when two rings of intense winds
form around the center, influencing TC evolution. This study examines how CEs affect IKE, a measure of
overall TC circulation. Using AI‐based data, we analyzed TC IKEs derived from satellite imagery. These IKEs
match aircraft observations with high accuracy (R = 0.996). Subsequently, we examine the CEs' impact, and
results reveal that long‐lived CEs (25% of cases) promote IKE growth, leading to larger storms with extensive
circulation. By mapping these changes on intensity‐energy diagrams, we show that CEs substantially influence
the TC life cycle.
1. Introduction
Theoretical modeling (Hack & Schubert, 1986; Musgrave et al., 2012; Ooyama, 1969; Smith et al., 2021) of
idealized, axisymmetric tropical cyclones (TCs) has significantly advanced our understanding of TC life cycles.
Despite their simplifications, these models effectively capture the structural evolution of TCs, including intensity
(Vmax), radius of maximum wind, minimum surface pressure, radius of gale‐force winds, and integrated kinetic
energy (IKE). Intensity, often represented by maximum sustained winds, is a limited metric for understanding the
full spatial structure and destructive potential of TCs, including their impact on storm surge and feedback to
systems of larger scales. To address this, IKE has been proposed, combining both a storm's intensity and size
(Irish, 2008; Klotzbach et al., 2022; Lavender & McBride, 2021; Maclay et al., 2008; Powell & Reinhold, 2007).
Powell and Reinhold (2007) and Maclay et al. (2008) suggested a two‐parameter storm classification system
based on Vmax and IKE due to the significant variability of IKE at a given Vmax.
Musgrave et al. (2012) employed K‐V diagrams to summarize TC structural evolutions, depicting the relationship
between Vmax and the IKE within a 1,000‐km radius and surface to 10 km height based on Ooyama's model
simulation. Musgrave et al. (2012) outlined a typical TC life cycle, beginning with an incipient stage where IKE
and Vmax slowly increase until Vmax reaches 25 m s−1. This is followed by a deepening stage with a more rapid
increase in both IKE and Vmax until Vmax exceeds 60 m s−1. Finally, in the mature stage, IKE steadily grows while
Vmax stabilizes or decreases.
A notable source of the variability in a K‐V diagram is the occurrence of concentric eyewalls (CE), where storm
intensification is interrupted by the formation of an outer eyewall, the decay of the inner eyewall, and the
contraction of the outer eyewall (Didlake & Houze, 2011; Houze et al., 2007; Rozoff et al., 2008; Terwey &
Montgomery, 2008; Willoughby et al., 1982; Yang et al., 2013). Sitkowski et al. (2011) indicated that CEs could
increase TC size as the inner‐core structure undergoes dramatic changes, resulting in intensity oscillations and
rapid wind field broadening.

Yang et al. (2013, 2021) identified CEs from 1997 to 2014, examining their duration and structural parameters,
including inner eyewall size, moat width, and outer eyewall width. Differences in these parameters can be best
distinguished by short‐lived and long‐lived CEs, with long‐lived CEs exhibiting larger moat and outer eyewall
widths while maintaining similar inner eyewall sizes compared to short‐lived CEs. However, the impact of short‐
lived and long‐lived CEs on TC circulation, particularly inner‐core and outer‐region IKE, remains unclear due to
the limited observation of TC IKEs.
An opportunity at this moment to further explore the relationships between CEs and TC development pathways,
as revealed by K‐V diagrams, is the emergence of AI or deep learning techniques for TC analysis and forecasting.
Deep learning techniques have presented opportunities to advance TC forecasting by learning from extensive
historical satellite images; they have been successfully applied to TC formation and center positioning (Matsuoka
et al., 2018), TC intensity estimation (Chen et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020; Higa et al., 2021; Wimmers
et al., 2019), TC size and structure estimation (Chen, Chen, & Chen, 2021; Chen et al., 2023; Wang & Li, 2023;
Zhuo & Tan, 2021, 2023), and TC intensity forecasting (Chen et al., 2023). Notably, Chen et al. (2025) used Deep
Learning for the Structure Analysis of Typhoons (DSAT) model to construct an objective global TC wind profile
data set spanning 1981 to 2020. This model converts multichannel satellite imagery into a 0–750 km wind profile
of axisymmetric surface winds concerning the TC center. Of note, although AI‐based approaches can be widely
applied in TC research, many studies rely on TC best‐tracks as labeled data. However, the reliability of best‐track
data varies across situations, making verification essential before using it to study scientific questions.
This study examines the effects of long‐lived and short‐lived CEs on the TC development pathways based on
observational data, as revealed by K‐V diagrams, particularly after the lifetime maximum intensity (LMI). Do
CEs play a critical role in modulating the pathway of the TC lifecycle? We first extend the western North Pacific
(WNP) CE data set to 2020, following the methods of Yang et al. (2013, 2021). Then, we combine these CE
records with DSAT TC wind profiles (Chen et al., 2025) to investigate the relationship among CEs, Vmax,
and IKE.
2. Extension of the Concentric Eyewall Data Set (2007–2020)
Yang et al. (2013, 2021) utilized satellite passive microwave (PMW) brightness temperature (TB) data from ice‐
scattering channels (i.e., 85–92 GHz) to identify CEs from 1997 to 2014. This study extends the WNP CE data set
to 2020.
Following their method, PMW observations from the Special Sensor Microwave Imager (SSM/I), the Global
Precipitation Measurement (GPM) Microwave Imager (GMI), the Special Sensor Microwave Imager/Sounder
(SSM/IS), and the Advanced Microwave Scanning Radiometer 2 (AMSR‐2) were utilized to identify CE cases.
These satellite images were obtained from the Naval Research Laboratory (NRL), Monterey, California (Hawkins
et al., 2001, 2006) and downloaded from the NRL TC webpage. As shown in Figure 1a, the PMW TB observation
can effectively capture the inner eyewall, moat, and outer eyewall of Typhoon Mangkhut (2018).
This study objectively examines the TB radial profile across eight 45° azimuthal sectors to the TC center
(Figure 1b) to identify CE cases.
1. Locate the moat in each profile (Figure 1b) associated with a local maximum TB (indicative of weak con-
vection) between two TB local minima (indicative of strong convection).
2. Verify that the two eyewalls exhibit well‐identified convection and satisfy the criteria:
TB max ≧σouter min + TB outer min and TB max ≧σinner min + TB inner min,
where the TB_inner_min, TB_max, and TB_outer_min represent the minimum TB of the inner convection, the local
maximum TB within the possible moat, and the minimum TB of the possible outer eyewall. Furthermore, σinner_min
and σouter_min represent the standard deviation of the minimum TB accompanied by the possible inner and outer
eyewalls.
3. Confirm that TB_outer_min ≤230 K to ensure strong outer convection.
4. Ensure that at least five out of eight sectors meet the above criteria (1–3) to confirm a symmetric CE structure.

After identifying the presence of CEs in each PMW image, the CE duration is defined as the period from the first
to the last CE image. In addition, to avoid misclassifying multiple CEs as a single long‐lived CE due to inadequate
temporal resolution, the CE must maintain a consistent inner core size.
We used the above method to identify 30 CEs from 23 Typhoons during 2015–2020. As a result, during 1997–
2020, there were 78 short‐lived CEs and 26 long‐lived CEs, classified by the 20‐hr CE duration criterion (Yang
et al., 2021). Table S1 in Supporting Information S1 provides the list of all CEs from 1997 to 2020.
Consistent results are found for CEs using both the old (Yang et al., 2021) and new datasets. Differences in CE
structural parameters are clearly distinguished by CE duration. Long‐lived CEs (duration ≥20 h) exhibit wider
moats and larger outer eyewall widths compared to short‐lived CEs. Furthermore, TC tracks with long‐lived CEs
are typically located south of 25°N and west of the basins, exhibiting a lower northward translation speed
(Figure 1c, red tracks). Long‐lived CEs rarely move northeast into the mid‐latitude westerlies, characterized by
large and detrimental vertical shear. Conversely, TC tracks with CE durations < 20 h tend to have more significant
northward components (Figure 1c, light blue tracks). This highlights the importance of environmental factors in
the development of long‐lived CEs. Finally, no significant trends or statistical differences in CE characteristics
are found between the periods 1997–2008 and 2009–2020.

3. Deep Learning DSAT IKE Estimates
This study used the DSAT TC structure reanalysis (Chen et al., 2025) to examine the effect of a CE on the IKE
evolution of the TC. The deep learning DSAT model converts multichannel satellite imagery into a 0–750‐km
wind profile of axisymmetric surface winds for global TCs. Their effort yielded a 40‐year (1981–2020) ho-
mogenized data set, offering an opportunity to explore the structural development, alongside intensification, of
historical TCs. Notably, a unique labeling procedure is adopted to enable DSAT to end‐to‐end transform satellite
imagery into wind profiles. The labeled data are generated using a parametric model, which integrates the best
track intensity, radius of 34‐kt winds, and radius of maximum winds. Furthermore, outer (>∼300 km) surface
wind labeled data are from the numerical model reanalysis.
The DSAT model features a hybrid GAN‐CNN architecture (Chen, Chen, & Hsiao, 2021), incorporating a
generative adversarial network (GAN, Goodfellow et al., 2020) module and a polar convolutional neural network
(CNN, Chen, Chen, & Hsiao, 2021; Krizhevsky et al., 2012) regressor. The DSAT GAN module is trained to
generate synthetic VIS images (VIS*) based on all‐time available infrared (IR) and water vapor (WV) images,
enabling the indirect use of original VIS images to train the CNN regressor and achieve better accuracy. On the
other hand, the CNN regressor employs a “polar‐convolution filter” design accounting for TCs' rotational nature
(Chen, Chen, & Hsiao, 2021) to regress to labeled wind profiles of azimuthally averaged winds.
Statistical verification of the DSAT performance and comparisons to operational baselines (Chen et al., 2025,
their Figure 10) indicate that the DSAT Vmax estimates exhibit comparable accuracy to SATCON (Velden &
Herndon, 2014) and outperform MTCSWA (Knaff et al., 2011) for R34 (radius of 34‐kt winds) estimates.
Although MTCSWA is recognized as the primary operational tool for estimating R34, it has deficiencies when
polar‐orbiting satellite observations, such as Advanced Scatterometer (ASCAT) winds, are unavailable. In
contrast, DSAT provides stable estimates, resulting in better overall performance.
This study further examines the feasibility of using DSAT data for IKE analysis. First, we calculate 0–750‐km
IKE representing the overall TC energy/circulation:

As depicted in Figure 2c, DSAT data offer an efficient way to monitor Vmax and IKE simultaneously. For instance,
Hurricane Patricia (2015) rapidly intensified to record‐breaking intensity but did not substantially grow in IKE. In
contrast, Hurricane Katrina (2005) and Typhoon Hagibis (2020) exhibited extremely high IKE. This study also
verifies the DSAT estimates of IKE over the 200–750 km annulus (K200–750) and the DSAT surface wind speed at
the 500‐km radius (V500), a key parameter for describing TC outer winds (Knaff et al., 2014), using 465 ASCAT
TC observations provided by Chen et al. (2025). Results show that DSAT achieves strong correlations of R = 0.83
for V500 and R = 0.88 for K200–750 (Figures 2d and 2e). These results suggest that DSAT data are also valid for
examining the evolution of IKE in the TC outer‐core region. Note, however, that the DSAT model is limited to
geostationary satellite images, which may hinder its ability to detect CEs. Additionally, it is trained on wind
profiles lacking double wind peaks, thus necessitating further investigation into IKE evolution during the eyewall
replacement cycle. Nevertheless, despite these limitations, the DSAT data could be utilized to examine TC IKE
before and after the occurrence of CEs using statistical approaches.

4. TC Development Pathways in K‐V Diagrams and CEs' Impact
In an idealized simulation with a constant favorable environment (e.g., Musgrave et al., 2012), it has been
observed that a TC usually reaches its lifetime maximum intensity (LMI) before attaining its lifetime maximum
size or lifetime maximum IKE (LME). However, it is not the case for real TCs, which could be affected by an
unfavorable environment for development.

between LMI and LME. In contrast, short‐lived CE cases exhibit an average
delay of ∼24 hr, while long‐lived CE cases show a more significant delay.
This indicates that CEs may contribute to the delay between LME and LMI.
Thus, it is suggested that CE formation could be a critical process contributing
to the large counter‐clockwise developmental pathways of TCs, with which a
TC evolves from a “strong dwarf” to a “super giant,” then to a “weak giant,”
before their ultimate demise in the K‐V diagram shown in Musgrave
et al. (2012).
Although Figure 3b shows that CEs clearly contribute to longer LME delays,
the intense no‐CE cases still exhibit a slight delay. Results from Smith
et al. (2021) may explain this behavior. Using idealized cloud‐resolving
simulations, they demonstrated that a TC can continue expanding in size
even after reaching peak intensity, without undergoing an eyewall replace-
ment cycle. Their findings suggest that when eyewall convection becomes
inefficient at ventilating inward mass upward, frictional effects induce a spin‐
down process just above the boundary layer. This halts further intensification
while allowing outer convection to continue driving storm growth. A key
contribution of our study is linking this type of “outer convection” behavior to
the frequent occurrence of CEs in real‐world TCs.
Composite analysis is used to investigate the impact of CEs on TC devel-
opment pathways. We collected data from three subsets described in the
previous section: long‐lived CE cases, short‐lived CE cases, and a subset of
TCs without CEs. Figure 4 presents composites aligned with both the CE
formation time (Figures 4a–4d) and TC LMI (Figures 4e–4h). For the WNP
TCs, note that 48% of severe typhoons (Vmax > 96 kt) experience at least one
CE during their lifetime. Moreover, long‐lived CEs, comprising approxi-
mately 26% of all CE cases, promote the growth of TC IKE.
This study suggests that long‐lived CEs tend to maintain TC intensity after the CE formation time (Figure 4a) and

5. Summary and Discussion
Concentric eyewalls are a significant source of variability in TC structure, and
this paper first extends observational CE data up to 2020. Subsequently, we
leveraged the comprehensive CE data set and AI‐based TC wind profile
reanalysis to examine how CEs impact TC development pathways. This paper
employs K‐V diagrams to analyze TC development with CE TCs. The K‐V
diagram summarizes the time evolution of TC structure, providing a
comprehensive view of TC dynamics. This study explores the impact of long‐
lived and short‐lived CEs on TC development pathways compared to the no‐
CE TCs.
We assessed the effects of CEs on TC intensity, inner‐core IKE, outer‐region
IKE, and total IKE. This study reveals that short‐lived CEs (lasting less than
20 hr) help maintain TC IKE. In contrast, long‐lived CEs (comprising 25% of
all CEs) contribute to significant IKE growth, resulting in TCs with huge
circulations. Our analysis of long‐lived CE K‐V relationships generally aligns
with theoretical calculations, except during the decaying stage. In idealized
simulations (Musgrave et al., 2012; Ooyama, 1969), a TC never enters an
environment unfavorable to its development, so the IKE keeps growing after
the LMI. On the contrary, about half of real‐world severe typhoons not asso-
ciated with CEs exhibit decreasing IKE and Vmax simultaneously after entering
an unfavorable environment. For the rest half of the severe typhoons with CEs,
CEs may modulate the TC life cycle, evolving from a “strong dwarf” to a
“super giant,” and then to a “weak giant,” before their ultimate demise.
While we identify clear differences in IKE behavior, further investigation into
the underlying dynamical mechanisms is essential. Here, we propose a
conjecture: since long‐lived CEs tend to feature a larger moat and a broader
outer eyewall (Yang et al., 2021), this structure may enable stronger
dynamical efficiency (Kuo et al., 2019), leading to enhanced IKE generation
associated with outer eyewall convection. This increased efficiency may be
linked to heating within a relatively inertially stable region, where the broader
secondary eyewall contributes more effectively to IKE growth than typical
rainband‐related heating. Future work will incorporate idealized numerical
modeling and theoretical diagnostics to better understand how CE duration
influences outer‐core winds and IKE evolution.
Integrating observational CE data with AI‐based TC wind profiles, IKE en-
hances our understanding of how CEs affect TC development pathways,
elucidating the mechanisms behind K‐V relationships. Future work could also
benefit from expanding the CE database by incorporating more cases or
additional basins, as well as leveraging high‐resolution reanalysis and satellite
